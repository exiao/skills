---

## The Core Principle

The first 1–3 seconds decide everything.

We live in a scroll-first world. Your goal is to **interrupt the scroll. Interrupt the pattern.**

People stop when something in that first 1–3 seconds made their brain pause. A strong hook doesn't beg for attention — it interrupts patterns.

**Attention is earned, not assumed.**

---

## Dual-Hook Rule

Every strong video needs **both**:
1. **Visual hook** — something visually disruptive in the first frame(s)
2. **Spoken/text hook** — the opening line or on-screen text

Don't rely on just one. Use both.

---

## 5 Spoken/Text Hook Types

Use one of these as your opening line or first on-screen text:

| Type | What it does | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Call out a specific person** | Makes the viewer feel directly addressed | "If you're a solo founder still doing X..." |
| **Highlight a pain point** | Triggers recognition and stops the scroll | "This is why your portfolio keeps bleeding..." |
| **Bold or controversial statement** | Creates friction, demands a response | "Dollar-cost averaging is making you poor." |
| **Curiosity gap** | Withholds the payoff, forces the watch | "The one chart that changed how I invest..." |
| **Clear outcome promise** | Transactional — here's what you'll get | "By the end of this, you'll know exactly when to sell." |

## Visual Hook Techniques (with reference frames)

Screenshots from: @ugc.withrach — `hook-frames/` folder

---

### 1. Kinetic Hair/Body Motion
**File:** `hook-frames/hook-hair-motion.jpg`
Hair dramatically fanned out mid-lift — the motion itself is the hook. Works because the eye chases movement.
Apply as: subject mid-action in frame 1, not posed static. Start the clip already moving.

---

### 2. Low Angle + Foreground Bokeh
**File:** `hook-frames/hook-low-angle-bokeh.jpg`
Camera below subject looking up, with blurred object (flowers) in the foreground. Creates depth and an unexpected POV.
Apply as: camera at waist/floor level, place a soft object (plant, product, hand) blurred in the lower foreground.

---

### 3. Hand Thrust Toward Camera
**File:** `hook-frames/hook-hand-to-camera.jpg`
Fist/hand reaches toward the lens, partially blocking the face. Breaks the fourth wall, feels direct and personal.
Apply as: subject extends hand toward camera in first 1–2 seconds before pulling back to speak.

---

### 4. Camera Spin / Motion Blur
**File:** `hook-frames/hook-motion-blur-spin.jpg`
Rapid camera rotation during the opening — everything blurs, then resolves. Pure pattern interrupt.
Apply as: start clip mid-spin, let it settle into the talking-head frame. Feels like you're jumping into the middle of something.

---

### 5. Peek Around Object / Door
**File:** `hook-frames/hook-peek-around-door.jpg`
Subject peeking around edge of a door, smiling. Low angle. Creates curiosity + warmth simultaneously.
Apply as: shoot from low angle with a door or wall edge as a reveal device. Subject appears, then steps into frame.

---

### 6. Full Body + Environmental Context
**File:** `hook-frames/hook-full-body-doorway.jpg`
Instead of a close-up talking head, subject is shown full-body in a doorway with a prop (spray bottle). Provides visual context before a word is spoken.
Apply as: pull back to full body for the opening frame when the topic has a physical/location context. Then cut to close-up for the talk.

---

### 7. Hair Flip Transition
**File:** `hook-frames/hook-hair-flip-transition.jpg`
Mid-hair-flip: hair dramatically spread wide, arms slightly out, direct eye contact. The contrast between the chaos of the hair and the calm eye contact is the hook.
Apply as: cut TO this frame as the hook opener — hair already in motion, eye contact maintained.

---

## Testing Protocol

If a video underperforms, these are the levers (in order):

1. Change the opening sentence / first 3 seconds only
2. Change the on-screen text hook
3. Repost with a stronger first line — same content can succeed with a better hook
4. The content might be fine — it just never got a fair chance

## The 6 Annotated Techniques (with reference frames)

### 1. Curiosity Hook (Opening)
**File:** `hook-frames/chung/chung-02-curiosity-hook.jpg`
Label: **"Curiosity hook"**
Her opening line: *"Have you ever wondered whether other people see color the same way as you?"*
Why it works: asks a question everyone has thought but never looked up. Instantly personal. No one scrolls past their own unresolved curiosity.
Apply as: open with a question the viewer has definitely wondered but never answered. Make it feel like you're reading their mind.

---

### 2. Credibility Drop
**File:** `hook-frames/chung/chung-03-credibility.jpg`
Label: **"for credibility"**
Her line: *"I used to work in a research lab"* — delivered early, almost casually.
Why it works: credibility before the claim. You buy the answer because you trust the source. It's 5 words.
Apply as: slip credentials in early and fast — not a formal intro, just a casual detail that reframes everything after it.

---

### 3. Loop Opening
**File:** `hook-frames/chung/chung-04-opens-new-loop.jpg`
Label: **"opens new loop"**
Her line: *"so I'll tell you why I used to work in a research lab"* — opens a second thread inside the first.
Why it works: the viewer is now tracking two open questions simultaneously. They can't leave because they'd lose both answers.
Apply as: after establishing the main hook, open a sub-question that depends on hearing more. Stack loops, don't just open one.

---

### 4. Curiosity Peaks (Mid-video retention)
**File:** `hook-frames/chung/chung-05-peaks-curiosity.jpg`
Label: **"peaks curiosity"**
She introduces the science of color-detecting cells — specific and surprising enough to maintain interest mid-video.
Why it works: curiosity isn't one hit, it's sustained. Every 10–15 seconds something new should make the viewer lean in.
Apply as: plan a "curiosity peak" every ~10s. New angle, surprising fact, specific detail — something that wasn't predictable from the setup.

---

### 5. Dead Space Trim
**File:** `hook-frames/chung/chung-06-dead-space.jpg`
Label: **"all dead space"**
Chung highlights a segment she cuts entirely — no value, no movement forward.
Why it works: dead space is where people scroll. Editing is a hook technique. Every second that doesn't pull forward is a drop risk.
Apply as: after recording, watch back and mark every moment where nothing new is introduced. Cut it. If you're explaining something you've already established — cut it.

---

### 6. Obvious Take (Re-engagement)
**File:** `hook-frames/chung/chung-07-obvious-take.jpg`
Label: **"obvious take"**
She states something plainly relatable near the end.
Why it works: after a lot of complexity, giving viewers a moment where they nod along ("yes, exactly") re-engages them. It's a trust restore before the close.
Apply as: after dense or surprising content, land one beat that's obvious and agreeable. Lets the viewer feel smart, not lost.

# Source 3: @ErnestoSOFTWARE — Power Word Hook + Carousel Format

Original post: https://x.com/ErnestoSOFTWARE/status/2028176460293317018
Screenshots: `hook-frames/ernesto/`

The case study: TikTok carousel by @levelup.exe titled **"the illegal formula to wake up at 4:30 am (that actually works)"** — 1.4M views, 121.3K likes, 88.4K saves → promoted the Pillow sleep tracker app → 70K downloads + $300K revenue in one month.

One word made the difference.

---

## The Core Technique: Power Word Hook

**File:** `hook-frames/ernesto/ernesto-01-power-word-hook.jpg`

The rewrite:
- ❌ "here's how to wake up at 4:30AM everyday"
- ✅ "the **illegal** formula to wake up at 4:30 am (that actually works)"

Why "illegal" works: it implies the viewer is getting access to something they normally couldn't — a shortcut, a cheat, something gatekept. The word creates a miniature forbidden-fruit frame around completely normal information.

**Power words that do the same job:**

| Category | Words |
|----------|-------|
| Forbidden frame | illegal, banned, forbidden, they don't want you to know, censored |
| Insider access | secret, leaked, hidden, underground, unreleased |
| Contradiction | actually works, that nobody talks about, you've been doing wrong |
| Specificity + intrigue | exact formula, the [X] method, step-by-step system |

Apply as: take any normal how-to title. Find the one word that makes it sound like the viewer is getting access to something they shouldn't have. Replace it.

---

## The Carousel / Slideshow Format

**File:** `hook-frames/ernesto/ernesto-02-carousel-content.jpg`

The carousel is a visual hook system — the title slide is the hook, and every subsequent slide is a retention mechanism.

Structure that drove 1.4M views:
1. **Slide 1 (hook):** "the illegal formula to wake up at 4:30 am, easily" — power word + specific promise + "easily" (reduces resistance)
2. **Content slides (2–10):** Numbered, specific, named steps ("the 15-minute sleep shift", "the red-light protocol") — each slide title is its own mini-hook
3. **Last slide:** CTA — leads to product in bio

Key: each slide name uses the same formula — **"the [specific name]"** — makes the content feel like a proprietary system, not generic advice.

---

## The Cause → Result Proof Format

**File:** `hook-frames/ernesto/ernesto-03-revenue-proof.jpg`

Ernesto's video itself uses a visual hook format:

```
[1.4M VIEWS TikTok screenshot]  →  [App Stats: 70K downloads, $300K revenue]
       "1.4M VIEWS" badge              "$300K/MO APP" label
```

The format proves the claim before you've heard it. No narration needed — the viewer reads the two numbers and understands the argument in under 2 seconds.

Apply as: when making a proof/results video, lead with the outcome visible in the first frame. Don't build up to it — show it immediately, then explain how.
