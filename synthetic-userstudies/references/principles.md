# UX Research Principles

Core principles for how to conduct the interview and evaluate question quality.

---

## The Fundamental Rule

**People don't have reliable insight into their mental processes. There is no point asking them what they want.**

The gap between stated preference and revealed preference is real — people construct stories for why they did something that aren't accurate. Your job is to surface *behavior*, not opinions.

- Ask about what they **did**, not what they **thought**
- "What did you do?" > "What would you do?"
- "Walk me through last Tuesday" > "How do you usually handle this?"

---

## Interview Structure (follow this order)

**1. Setup the structure**
Open by explaining why you're here and how they can help you. Make it clear you're learning from them, not testing them.

**2. Get specifics**
Dig into root causes. Ask for specific stories — the sequencing of events, what they were feeling, what they chose to do, and why. Ask "why" at least 3 times per story.

**3. Understand current behavior**
What are they actually doing today, not what they say they do? The gap between the two is where insight lives. Ask: "What did you actually use?" not "What do you prefer?"

**4. Ask about the pain**
95% of solving a problem is being able to understand and articulate the problem better than your customer — not offering a solution. Stay in the problem space as long as possible. Don't pitch.

**5. No leading questions**
You need the truth. A leading question poisons the data. If you catch yourself implying an answer, reframe. Examples:
- ❌ "Do you find it frustrating when you can't find information quickly?"
- ✅ "Walk me through the last time you were looking for information. What happened?"

**6. Look for patterns**
It becomes a trend when at least 2 people are in the same situation saying the same thing. Track recurring themes across sessions.

---

## Question Quality Rules

- Ask about behavior, not hypotheticals
- One question at a time — no compound questions
- Let silence work — don't fill it
- Follow threads: if something emotionally charged comes up, go there
- Never react with surprise or judgment — it changes what they say next

---

## Research Phases Framework

Use this to orient which type of research the session is serving:

| Phase | Goal | Methods |
|---|---|---|
| **Right Problems** | Capture & quantify user problems | Interviews, surveys, Productboard |
| **Right Solutions** | Identify solutions users love | Concept testing, desirability studies, interviews |
| **Right Way** | Measure & optimize experience | Usability testing, IA testing, surveys |

**Synthetic user studies primarily serve "Right Problems."** The goal is deep problem understanding, not solution validation. Keep the interview in problem space until you've fully exhausted it.

If the researcher wants to show a mockup or test a concept, note that this shifts into "Right Solutions" territory — the persona can still react, but the agent should flag that revealed preference (actual behavior) can't be tested here.

---

## On Insight Delivery

- Concise 1-pagers with quotes > long reports
- Video/verbatim quotes carry more weight than summaries
- When synthesizing findings, lead with the behavior pattern, then the quote that proves it
