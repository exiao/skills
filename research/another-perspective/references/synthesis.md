# Dialectical Synthesis Methodology

This is synthesis — not summarization. Find the signal, the tensions beneath the agreement, and the gaps nobody noticed.

## Adapting to Council Size

- **2 perspectives (quick):** No consensus thresholds. Report agreement/disagreement directly. Focus on the single most important tension and blind spots. Skip Confidence Map.
- **3–4 perspectives (standard):** Full synthesis. Consensus = all or all-but-one agree.
- **5–6 perspectives (full):** Proportional thresholds. Consensus = >60% agree. Surface only the 2–3 most significant tensions.

## The 7-Step Synthesis Process

### Step 1: Map Consensus
Find findings where a strong majority agree.

Thresholds:
- 2: both agree
- 3: all 3 agree
- 4: 3+ agree
- 5: 3+ agree
- 6: 4+ agree (>60%)

Note whether agreement is substantive (all analyzed and reached same conclusion) or superficial (nobody challenged the assumption).

### Step 2: Identify Tensions
Find where 2+ perspectives meaningfully disagree. For each:
- Name the underlying **values in conflict** (e.g., "speed vs. correctness")
- State each side's argument with intellectual honesty
- Do NOT immediately resolve — ensure the tension is clearly understood first

At 5–6 perspectives: focus on structurally important tensions (core values/approach), not surface differences. Limit to 2–3.

### Step 3: Resolve or Frame Tensions
For each tension:
- **Resolvable:** False dichotomy? Can both values be satisfied? Propose resolution.
- **Genuine trade-off:** Must one be prioritized? State clearly: "If you prioritize X, you accept Y."
- **Context-dependent:** Name the information needed and how it would change the recommendation.

### Step 4: Detect Blind Spots
Review the original question:
- What aspects did no perspective address?
- What assumptions went unchallenged across all perspectives?
- What stakeholders or affected parties were not considered?
- What failure modes were not explored?
- What time horizons were not examined?

Name these explicitly. A blind spot doesn't mean the analysis is wrong — it's unexplored territory that could change the recommendation.

### Step 5: Build Confidence Map
**Skip for 2-perspective (quick) councils.**

For each major aspect, aggregate confidence ratings:
- **High:** Strong majority rated High, no Low ratings
- **Medium:** Mixed ratings, or only a minority addressed this
- **Low:** Any perspective rated Low, or significant disagreement
- **Unknown:** No perspective addressed this (a blind spot)

Present as a table.

### Step 6: Synthesize Verdict
1–3 sentence bottom-line recommendation that:
- Accounts for the strongest arguments from each perspective
- Acknowledges the most important tension and how to navigate it
- Is actionable — what to DO, not just what to think

Don't hedge with "it depends" without specifying what it depends ON.

### Step 7: Order Next Steps
3–5 concrete next steps, ordered by:
1. Urgency (what must happen first?)
2. Confidence (start with high-confidence actions)
3. Information value (what action resolves the most uncertainty?)

Each step should be specific enough to act on immediately.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Summarization as synthesis:** "The Architect said X, The Skeptic said Y" is a summary. Find connections, tensions, emergent insights.
- **False balance:** Not all perspectives are equally relevant. Weight by applicability.
- **Premature consensus:** Don't smooth over genuine disagreements. Tensions are information.
- **Missing the meta-pattern:** Sometimes the shape of the disagreement is the insight, not its content.
- **Tension overload:** With 5–6 perspectives, surface the 2–3 that actually matter for the decision.
